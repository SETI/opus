About OPUS

OPUS (Outer Planets Unified Search), a web-based data search tool for NASA outer planet missions, is a project of the Ring-Moon Systems Node of NASA's Planetary Data System (PDS), The PDS is a carefully-curated permanent archive of all types of mission data, which are freely available to researchers and the general public. The Ring-Moon Systems Node, one of eight Discipline Nodes, specializes in imaging, spectral, and occultation data related to the outer planets, rings, and moons of our solar system. It is hosted at The SETI Institute in Mountain View, California.

OPUS supports user-friendly searching on metadata normally associated with each observation, such as the mission, instrument, intended target, observation time, and PDS volume name, as well as metadata specific to each supported mission and instrument.

In addition, OPUS excels at multi-mission and multi-instrument searches by providing search parameters that are more generic in nature. Among these, which are computed by the Ring-Moon Systems Node and uniquely available in OPUS, are:

  • Ring Geometry, such as radius, longitude, resolution, and phase angle, calculated separately for each observation relative to the ring plane of the appropriate planet.
    For example, consider this query, which searches for:
    • Images taken by Cassini's ISS Narrow-Angle Camera
    • of the Keeler Gap in Saturn's rings (136,400-136,600 km)
    • at high resolution (< 2 km per pixel)
    • on the lit side of the rings (emission angle 0-75°)
  • Body Surface Geometry, such as longitude, latitude, distance, resolution, and phase angle, calculated separately for each observation for all bodies in the field of view.
    For example, consider this query, which searches for:
    • All observations of Pluto
    • at high phase (> 160°)
    • and high resolution (< 5 km per pixel)
  • Observation Type, such as "Image", "Spectrum", or "Spectral Time Series".
  • Measurement Quantity, such as "Reflectivity" or "Optical Depth".
  • Image attributes, such as size in pixels or number possible levels.
  • Wavelength attributes, such as wavelength range and resolution, which permit searching for observations in a particular bandpass across instruments and filters.

OPUS supplies data from the following missions and instruments:

  • Cassini (Flew past Jupiter in 2001, orbited Saturn from 2004-2017)
    • CIRS (Composite Infrared Spectrometer)
    • ISS (Imaging Science Subsystem) - the primary image cameras
    • UVIS (Ultraviolet Imaging Spectrograph)
    • VIMS (Visible and Infrared Mapping Spectrometer)
  • Galileo (orbited Jupiter from 1995-2003)
    • SSI (Solid-State Imaging) - the primary image camera
  • Voyager 1 and 2 (Flew past Jupiter in 1977-1978 and Saturn in 1980-1981; Voyager 2 continued past Uranus in 1986 and Neptune in 1989)
    • ISS (Imaging Science Subsystem) - the primary image cameras
  • New Horizons (flew past Jupiter in 2007, Pluto in 2016, and MU69 in 2019)
    • LORRI (Long Range Reconnaissance Imager) - the primary image camera
    • MVIC (Multispectral Visible Imaging Camera)
  • The Hubble Space Telescope (in Earth orbit)
    • ACS (Advanced Camera for Surveys)
    • NICMOS (Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer)
    • STIS (Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph)
    • WFC3 (Wide-Field Camera 3)
    • WFPC2 (Wide-Field Planetary Camera 2)
